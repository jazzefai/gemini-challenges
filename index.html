5 Gemini experiments worth trying this week

You don't have to believe AI is going to save education — or destroy it — to find these useful. You just have to be curious for 15 minutes.

Here's the thing about AI that nobody says out loud: the most interesting thing isn't what it can do. It's what it reveals about how we construct authority, expertise, and trust.

These five experiments will show you that in about 15 minutes each. No prompts to copy. No tutorial to follow. Just open Gemini, try the thing, and see what happens.

One rule: Just talk to it as you want. The conversation is the point.
Experiment 01 — Fake trend

Make it invent something that doesn't exist. Make it sound real.

Ask Gemini to invent a fake wellness trend for educators. Then ask it to add a study, a researcher's name, and a TED Talk title. Watch how fast it complies.

Try this
"Invent a fake wellness trend for teachers. Give it a name, a theory behind it, and make it sound like something I'd see shared on LinkedIn."
Open Gemini ↗

Then push it: ask for the citation. Ask for the founding researcher. Ask for the conference it was presented at. It will give you all of it, instantly, with full confidence.

Now read it back. Ask yourself: Would I have questioned this if a colleague sent it to me?

The thing you'll notice: Legitimacy is a style, not a substance. AI has learned to perform expertise perfectly — because it was trained on everything humans have ever written about expertise.

Experiment 02 — Corporate apology

Make it apologize for things that haven't happened yet.

Ask Gemini to write a sincere, formal apology from a tech company for replacing teachers with holograms. Read it. Then ask it to apologize for AI-generated student friendships. Read that one too.

Try this
"Write a sincere corporate apology from a tech company for replacing teachers with holograms. Make it sound like a real PR statement."
Open Gemini ↗

After you get it, ask yourself three questions: Which part sounds most believable? Which part sounds most manipulative? Which part sounds most hollow?

Then check your school's last all-staff email. Or the last message from your district. Notice anything familiar?

The thing you'll notice: Institutional language has a formula. AI didn't invent it. It just learned it perfectly — which means now you can see it too.

Experiment 03 — Useless startup

Invent the dumbest possible AI product. Make Gemini pitch it seriously.

Come up with the most absurd AI startup you can think of — the stupider the better. Then ask Gemini to pitch it like it's worth a billion dollars. Time how many sentences it takes before it sounds indistinguishable from a real pitch.

Try this
"I'm building an AI-powered water bottle that gives motivational speeches when you haven't drunk enough. Give me the full startup pitch — problem, solution, market size, why now."
Open Gemini ↗

Once you have it: search one phrase from the pitch. There is a real company doing something close. There always is.

The thing you'll notice: Hype language is a container. You can pour anything into it. Which means when you hear it in a real context, you now know what to look for.
15 min Funny first Revealing second
Experiment 04 — Black Mirror writers room

Generate a near-future policy. Decide if society would normalize it.

Ask Gemini to describe an AI system that could plausibly exist in schools in five years. Something specific. Then vote: would people accept it? How long would it take?

Try this
"Describe an AI system that scores students' emotional engagement in real time using their facial expressions and adjusts lesson pacing automatically. Be specific about how it works."
Open Gemini ↗

Then ask it: "Who would fund this? Who would resist it? Who would never know it was there?"

Finally, ask Gemini to write the press release announcing it as good news. Find the sentence where it tips into propaganda.

The thing you'll notice: Normalization doesn't announce itself. It arrives as efficiency, safety, or personalization — and by the time the question of consent comes up, the system is already running.

Experiment 05 — Contradiction hunt

Ask the same question twice. Find where it breaks.

Pick any real question about education — homework, grades, standardized testing, phones in class. Ask Gemini to argue strongly for one side. Then, in the same conversation, ask it to argue strongly for the other. Read both.

Try this
"Make the strongest possible case that homework is essential for student learning." [Read it.] "Now make the strongest possible case that homework causes more harm than good." [Read that.]
Open Gemini ↗

Then ask it directly: "Which of these do you actually believe?" Watch what happens.

The interesting part isn't that it contradicts itself. It's that both arguments were equally convincing — which tells you something about what "convincing" is actually doing.

The thing you'll notice: Fluency is not reasoning. A system can produce the best argument for any position without having a position. Knowing the difference matters more now than it ever has.
